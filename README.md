# Smart Home Security Product and Supply Chain Strategy

An academic case study examining how a distributed wireless home-security concept could move from customer needs and early product design into demand planning and supply-chain strategy.

> This was a team project completed for TIM 172P and TIM 172Q at UC Santa Cruz. It was an academic exercise and was not sponsored or endorsed by Honeywell. Classmates' names and course materials are intentionally omitted.

## The Challenge

The project explored two connected questions:

1. What should a differentiated, distributed wireless home-security system offer customers?
2. What product, demand, and supply-chain decisions would be required to bring that concept to market?

The work began with customer needs such as safety, dependable alerts, ease of installation, device integration, battery reliability, and wireless range. It then translated those needs into technical requirements and an operating strategy.

## Product Strategy and Concept Development

The team used a structured product-development process to move from a broad problem to a selected concept:

- Compared existing home-security systems and mapped their core functions
- Used FAST diagrams and functional decomposition to connect user needs with system behavior
- Applied House of Quality to relate customer priorities to technical measures
- Generated six concepts using a morphological matrix
- Evaluated the alternatives through a weighted utility model

The selected concept was a distributed wireless system in which connected sensors could operate without relying on a single central controller. The concept prioritized flexible installation, redundancy, wireless connectivity, and continued operation during failures.

## Market and Supply-Chain Strategy

The second phase connected the selected product concept to its commercial and operational requirements:

- Assessed the home-security market and competitive landscape
- Applied Porter's Five Forces and competitive-positioning frameworks
- Mapped customer-order, replenishment, manufacturing, and procurement cycles
- Examined product, information, and cash flows across the supply chain
- Considered the balance between responsiveness and efficiency during launch
- Estimated two years of quarterly demand using market growth, seasonality, home-purchase activity, and property-risk indicators
- Used the demand outlook to inform forecasting and downstream planning

The analysis supported a responsive launch strategy. Because a new product begins with high demand uncertainty, the supply chain would initially favor flexibility and gradually become more efficient as adoption patterns became clearer.

I then translated the forecast into implications for launch planning, including why the supply chain should prioritize responsiveness while demand remained uncertain.

## What I Learned

This project showed me that product and operational decisions cannot be made separately. A promising concept still needs a credible market position, measurable customer value, realistic demand assumptions, and a supply chain capable of responding to uncertainty.

The most valuable lesson was learning how to turn an open-ended product problem into a sequence of defensible decisions. Rather than selecting a concept based only on intuition, we used customer needs, technical metrics, market evidence, forecasting, and weighted evaluation to explain why one direction was stronger than the alternatives.
